About

My mother was born during the Great Leap Forward in China. Contrary to the propoganda at the time, this was a time of great starvation and Xmillion deaths from severe resource misallocation by the CCP. Luckily my parents live now in a comfortable home in McLean, Virginia with a large backyard and ample resources. But my mother still goes to great lengths to conserve water for her organic garden. She uses extra water from the laundry machine to flush the toilets, and carries the water in buckets every day, like she did when she was young.

One day, while walking through our neighborhood, she saw a sprinkler watering a lawn, and said in astonishment, "I just can't believe the waste... They use water to grow the grass, and then they pay someobody to cut and take away the grass."

I realized she was absolutely right. The entire process was absurd and wasteful. But it's only wasteful if you know all the civil engineering infrastructure behind a water sprinkler. Without the basic awareness, homeowners have no idea - and are unable to make better decisions.

So what this company does, is it provides landscaping companies with resource efficiency certifications. Some companies completely avoid grass, and instead focus on gardens, mulch, stone, and natural fauna. But ultimately, it depends on the homeowner who decides how they want their home to be presented. By providing a certification process - along with a carefully designed marketing campaign and branding - we can allow homeowners to easily add in "sustainability" to their lawncare and design decisions.

Topographic Modeling

Saqsaywaman

Saqsaywaman is an Incan structure located 3,700 meters above sea level in Cusco, Peru. It boasts precise masonry of stones weighing over 2 tons and was built in the 1500s. Damaged by Spanish colonization and well-meaning archaeological practices, the site is unable to handle water runoff during the rainy season causing yearly degradation of the walls. This is a three dimensional topographic water model made from old maps and new survey points collected in the Summer of 2013.

  • Client: University of Virginia
  • Date: June 2013
  • Service: Historical Topographic Modeling
